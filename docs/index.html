For moments of anxiety

Come back to the present, one sense at a time.

Ballast guides the 5-4-3-2-1 senses exercise. Notice 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you feel, 2 you smell, and 1 you taste. Turn the Digital Crown after each item; your watch vibrates and advances automatically.

A concrete example

You feel anxious and open Ballast. What happens next?

The idea is simple: move your attention away from racing thoughts and toward what you can see, hear, and feel around you right now.

  1. 5
    SEE

    A window, a mug, your hand, a light, a chair.

  2. 4
    HEAR

    Traffic, air conditioning, your breathing, a bird.

  3. 3
    FEEL

    Your feet on the floor, the watch, fabric on your skin.

  4. 2
    SMELL

    Coffee and soap, for example.

  5. 1
    TASTE

    Water, tea, or simply the taste already in your mouth.

↻

After each item, turn the Digital Crown once. The number drops. When it reaches zero, the watch vibrates and the next sense appears.

A familiar five-senses technique

Simple enough to remember. Guided when remembering feels hard.

The 5-4-3-2-1 exercise is described by Harvard Health and NHS services as a way to bring attention back to the senses and the present moment. Ballast makes the sequence available on your wrist; it does not provide medical care.

Questions before installing

Ballast stays small on purpose.

What is 5-4-3-2-1?

A senses exercise: notice 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you feel, 2 you smell, and 1 you taste.

Do I need to watch the screen?

No. Haptics introduce every step, and the Digital Crown counts each item. The screen is a visual fallback.

Does it need an iPhone?

No companion app is required. After installation, Ballast runs independently on Apple Watch.

What if I cannot notice a smell or taste?

Notice what is already present, or recall a familiar smell or taste. There is no perfect answer and no score.

Private by design

Nothing leaves your watch.

Ballast has no account, analytics, advertising, health permissions, or backend. The MVP stores no exercise history.
